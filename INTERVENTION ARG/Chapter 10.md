# The Signal

The song uploaded at 2:13 a.m.

Nobody was waiting for it.

That was the first thing Nico got wrong.

He sat in the corner of his bedroom with one headphone cup over his left ear and the other pushed slightly back so he could hear whether the apartment had finally gone quiet. His monitor glowed blue against the wall. The room smelled like dust, stale coffee, and the warm plastic breath of machines that had been running too long.

On the desk were two empty cans, a MIDI keyboard with one broken key, an audio interface, a notebook full of titles he had never used, and a small cactus his sister had given him because she said even he could not kill a cactus.

The cactus was dying.

That felt personal.

Nico stared at the upload screen.

TITLE: lighthouse_for_no_one.wav

He hated the title.

Then loved it.

Then hated himself for caring.

The track was six minutes and forty-two seconds long, which was too long for people who skipped songs after fifteen seconds and too short for the version in his head that had started as a storm and ended as something closer to prayer.

It was not mixed right.

The low end was cloudy.

The snare sat weird.

The guitar part in the second half had a string squeak he should have edited out.

The vocal sample was probably too buried.

The whole thing leaned slightly left because one of his room reflections lied to him and his headphones lied differently.

He knew all of this.

Knowing did not help.

Knowing was how he had kept the song unreleased for seventeen days.

Seventeen days of tiny adjustments.

Half a decibel here.

Three milliseconds there.

Cut the reverb.

Add it back.

Mute the piano.

Unmute it.

Export.

Listen in the car.

Hate it.

Export again.

Listen on phone speakers.

Hate humanity.

Export again.

At some point, work stopped being improvement and became avoidance wearing producer clothes.

He knew that too.

The upload page waited.

A gray button at the bottom said POST.

Not release.

Not share.

Not begin.

Just POST.

A word so ordinary it seemed insulting.

Nico hovered the cursor over it.

He imagined the future.

Three listens.

One bot comment.

A friend saying fire bro without listening.

His cousin asking if he still made music.

A stranger skipping before the drums came in.

Nothing.

Worst of all, nothing.

Not hate.

Hate at least proved contact.

Nothing was a room with no echo.

His phone buzzed.

A text from his sister.

you still awake?

He looked at the time.

2:12.

He typed:

yeah

She replied immediately.

post the song coward

Nico stared.

Then laughed.

Not because it was funny.

Because she was a sniper.

He typed:

it’s not done

She replied:

neither are you. post it anyway.

He leaned back in the chair.

The cactus tilted accusingly.

The room was quiet now.

No footsteps upstairs.

No traffic outside except the occasional car passing like a thought that had somewhere else to be.

The project file was still open behind the browser.

Sixty-three tracks.

Half of them muted.

Five labeled final.

Three labeled real final.

One labeled final_final_please_stop.

A timeline full of automation curves and bad decisions.

A whole month of his life rendered into colored rectangles.

He clicked POST before he could form another committee.

The button turned gray.

A loading circle spun.

Then the screen said:

UPLOAD COMPLETE

The song existed somewhere other than his room.

That should have felt good.

It did not.

It felt like setting a paper boat in a gutter during rain and pretending it was a ship.

He refreshed the page.

Zero plays.

Of course.

He refreshed again.

Zero.

Again.

Zero.

His sister texted:

did you do it

He sent a screenshot.

She replied:

proud of you. go to sleep.

He did not go to sleep.

He watched the number.

At 2:26, it became one.

His heart kicked.

Then he realized it was probably him.

He had played it from the page to check the upload.

One.

Pathetic.

He closed the laptop.

Opened it.

Closed it again.

The screen went black, then flickered back on without permission.

White text appeared over his own reflection.

SIGNAL DETECTED

Nico froze.

The project file was gone.

The browser was gone.

Only black screen.

White letters.

ROOM: BEDROOM  
STATE: RELEASED  
LAYER: OUTPUT

He leaned forward.

“No.”

The screen changed.

OUTPUT HAS LEFT THE ROOM.

Nico looked around as if someone might be standing behind him with a camera.

No one.

The cactus remained unsupportive.

The screen updated.

WARNING:

THE CREATOR DOES NOT CONTROL THE RECEIVER.

He sat very still.

A strange anger rose in him.

Not fear.

Anger.

Because the sentence felt like someone taking away the only fantasy that made posting tolerable.

Of course he wanted to control the receiver.

Not in an evil way.

Just enough.

Enough to make them listen all the way through.

Enough to make them understand that the second drop was supposed to feel like headlights through rain.

Enough to make them notice the buried voice saying stay.

Enough to make them hear the guitar line as grief instead of noodling.

Enough to make the song matter in the exact shape he had intended.

The laptop text changed.

INTENTION IS NOT ARRIVAL.

He hated that one.

“Cool,” he said. “Thanks.”

The screen returned to normal.

The upload page.

One play.

No comments.

He closed the laptop for real.

This time it stayed closed.

Across the city, someone else was awake.

Her name was Avery, and she was not supposed to be.

She had school in four hours.

Her mother thought she was asleep.

Her best friend thought she was asleep.

Her group chat was dead except for one person in a different time zone sending memes nobody liked enough to answer.

Avery lay on her bedroom floor with her back against the carpet and her legs up the side of the bed. The position made no sense, which was why she liked it. Beds had expectations. Floors did not.

The ceiling above her had glow-in-the-dark stars she had put up when she was nine and refused to take down at seventeen because removing childhood decorations felt too much like agreeing to become someone she had not approved yet.

Her phone rested on her chest.

It rose and fell with her breathing.

Too fast.

She was not crying anymore.

That part had passed.

Now she was in the flat place after crying, where the body felt wrung out and the mind became terribly clear in the cruelest ways.

The fight downstairs had ended an hour ago.

Not ended.

Stopped making noise.

Her mother and stepfather did that sometimes.

They did not resolve.

They powered down.

Avery had stood in the hallway holding a glass of water while they used words like always and never and ungrateful and overreacting and your daughter and my house.

Your daughter.

My house.

She had gone back to her room before anyone noticed she was there.

That was a skill.

Not being noticed at the moment being noticed would turn you into evidence.

Her phone screen showed a music app she did not remember opening.

Autoplay had carried her somewhere strange.

A track title appeared.

lighthouse_for_no_one.wav

No artist picture.

No lyrics.

No comments.

One play.

Probably nothing.

She almost skipped it because the title was too on the nose and she hated when strangers accidentally named the room.

Then the first sound entered.

Not a beat.

A low hum.

Distant.

Like a refrigerator in an empty church.

Then piano.

Three notes.

Not pretty exactly.

Honest.

Avery stared at the ceiling.

The glow stars were faint now, almost out of charge.

The song moved slowly.

Too slowly for the part of her that wanted distraction.

But not too slowly for the part that was tired of being chased by noise.

A guitar arrived halfway through the first minute.

The string squeaked.

She heard it clearly.

For some reason, that was what kept her listening.

The mistake.

Or not mistake.

The human part.

Something about the squeak said a hand had been there.

A real hand.

A person touching a string in a room somewhere.

Not polished.

Not perfect.

Not content.

A signal.

Avery closed her eyes.

The buried voice came in at 2:11.

Almost hidden under reverb.

A single word.

Stay.

She opened her eyes.

The song did not know her.

That was important.

It had not been written to her.

It had not arrived with her name on it.

It had not said, Avery, this is for you.

That would have made her suspicious.

Direct comfort often felt like a trick.

But this was stranger comfort.

Safer.

A paper boat from a gutter somewhere, drifting under her door.

Stay.

The word repeated later, but differently.

Not as a command.

Not as a plea.

More like a light blinking on the shore of a place she could not see yet.

She put the phone on the floor beside her and let the track fill the room.

At 3:03, her bedroom lamp flickered.

Avery opened her eyes.

The bulb had been dying for weeks, so she ignored it.

The phone screen dimmed.

Then brightened.

The album art space, which had been blank, turned black.

White text appeared.

SIGNAL RECEIVED

Avery sat up.

The music kept playing.

The screen changed.

ROOM: BEDROOM FLOOR  
STATE: OVERLOADED  
LAYER: RECEIVER

She stared at it.

Her heart began moving faster.

The screen updated.

CURRENT SIGNAL CONTENT:

HUM  
PIANO  
STRING NOISE  
BURIED VOICE  
UNINTENDED MESSAGE  
HUMAN PRESENCE

Then:

RECEIVER INTERPRETATION:

SOMEONE ELSE WAS AWAKE.  
SOMEONE ELSE MADE A LIGHT.  
THE LIGHT WAS NOT MADE FOR ME.  
IT REACHED ME ANYWAY.

Avery’s mouth opened slightly.

The song swelled.

Not dramatically.

It did not become cinematic enough to embarrass itself.

It simply widened.

Drums entered like a pulse deciding to continue.

The phone text changed.

INTERVENTION POINT AVAILABLE:

DO NOT TRANSLATE TEMPORARY DARKNESS INTO TOTAL FUTURE.

Avery stopped breathing for a second.

The sentence did not say everything would be fine.

Good.

She would not have believed that.

It did not say her family would change.

It did not say school would be easier.

It did not say her mother would choose correctly.

It did not say the house would become safe just because a song had entered it.

It said one thing.

Do not turn this moment into all moments.

That was different.

That was possible.

She looked toward her bedroom door.

The house was quiet in the powered-down way.

Her phone buzzed.

Not from the app.

A message from her best friend.

you up?

Avery almost laughed.

It was 3:06 in the morning.

Of course the exact person who said she was going to sleep at midnight was now awake.

Avery typed:

yeah

Then stopped.

Usually she would send nothing else.

Or a joke.

Or a meme.

Or lie.

Tonight, the song kept moving.

The phone screen still showed white text over black.

CALL WITNESS

She typed:

can you stay on the phone for a minute?

The reply came fast.

calling

The phone rang.

The song lowered automatically under the call.

Her best friend’s voice came through, sleepy and worried.

“Hey.”

Avery pressed the phone to her ear and looked at the dying glow stars.

“Hey.”

“You okay?”

Avery stared at the ceiling.

No.

Maybe.

Not dead.

Not fine.

On floor.

Signal received.

She said, “I don’t want to be alone in my head right now.”

There was a small silence.

Then her friend said, “Okay. I’m here.”

The song ended at 6:42.

Autoplay tried to begin something louder.

Avery stopped it.

She saved the track.

Then, after a moment, shared it to her friend.

“What is this?” her friend asked.

“I don’t know,” Avery said. “A lighthouse, I guess.”

By morning, Nico’s song had seven plays.

He saw the number before brushing his teeth.

Seven.

He stared at it as if it had insulted him.

Seven was more than one.

Less than meaning.

He refreshed.

Still seven.

No comments.

No likes.

One save.

That was new.

One person had saved it.

He clicked the analytics tab.

Bad idea.

The graph was almost empty.

Two plays from his city.

Five from somewhere else.

One listener had played the song all the way through.

One.

He stared at that number longer than he wanted to admit.

Then the laptop flickered.

White text appeared.

SIGNAL PATH UPDATED

Below it was a map.

Not a real map.

No names.

No exact location.

Only points.

SOURCE ROOM: BEDROOM  
RECEIVER ROOM: UNKNOWN  
TRANSFER METHOD: AUTOPLAY  
INTENDED MESSAGE: UNRESOLVED  
RECEIVED MESSAGE: STAY

Nico’s skin prickled.

The text changed.

OUTPUT BECAME INPUT.

He read it again.

Output became input.

There was no applause in that sentence.

No proof of success.

No promise that the song was good.

No guarantee anyone would come back.

But someone had received something.

Not necessarily what he meant.

Maybe better.

Maybe worse.

Maybe simply different.

The screen updated.

CREATOR REQUEST:

CONTROL RECEPTION  
MEASURE IMPACT  
CONFIRM WORTH THROUGH RESPONSE

Then:

SYSTEM LIMIT:

THE SIGNAL DOES NOT RETURN WITH EVERY RIPPLE IT CREATES.

Nico sat back.

That one hurt.

Because he had wanted the ripple to come back wearing numbers.

Plays.

Likes.

Shares.

Comments.

Money.

Proof.

Something visible enough to hold up against the old voice that said nobody was waiting for what he made.

But maybe that was not how signals worked.

Maybe some signals did not return.

Maybe they entered a room, changed a decision by one degree, and disappeared into a life the creator would never know.

That felt unfair.

It also felt holy in a way he did not trust.

His sister texted at 8:14.

how many plays superstar

He replied:

7 lol

She sent:

that’s 7 more than 0

He stared at the message.

Then typed:

one save

She replied:

then somebody packed it in their bag

He smiled.

Packed it in their bag.

That was better than saved.

Saved sounded like a platform metric.

Packed it in their bag sounded like the song had become an object.

Something carried.

He looked at the dying cactus.

“Somebody packed it in their bag,” he told it.

The cactus did not care.

At school, Avery played the song once in the bathroom before first period.

Not because she wanted to be dramatic.

Because the hallway was loud and her chest was tight and the house was still in her skin.

She put one earbud in.

Not both.

Both ears in school meant vulnerability.

The hum began.

The piano entered.

The string squeak arrived.

She waited for it.

That was how quickly a mistake could become a landmark.

Her best friend came into the bathroom halfway through and leaned against the sink.

“Is that the lighthouse song?”

Avery nodded.

“It’s kind of weird,” her friend said.

“Yeah.”

“I like it.”

“Yeah.”

A girl in the stall flushed and came out.

“You guys gatekeeping bathroom depression music?”

Avery blinked.

Her friend laughed.

The girl washed her hands.

“What’s it called?”

Avery hesitated.

Then showed her.

lighthouse_for_no_one.wav

The girl looked at the screen.

“That’s depressing.”

“Yeah.”

“Send it.”

Avery did.

By lunch, the song had eleven saves.

Nico noticed during his break at work.

He worked in a print shop where machines larger than refrigerators ate paper and produced flyers nobody wanted. He wore earplugs most of the day and still heard the printers in his dreams.

At 12:33, he opened the analytics page behind the stack cutter where his manager could not see.

Plays: 31  
Saves: 11  
Shares: 4

He frowned.

That was not viral.

That was not even impressive.

But it was alive.

The laptop was not with him, only his phone.

Still, the screen flickered.

For half a second, the analytics page turned black.

INTERVENTION

Then:

RIPPLE STATUS: TRAVELING

He looked over his shoulder.

Nobody.

The page returned to normal.

He closed it.

Opened it again.

Still normal.

His manager yelled, “Nico! You on break or hiding?”

“Both!”

“Pick one!”

“Break!”

“Then stop hiding weird!”

Nico put the phone away.

The signal traveled.

Not cleanly.

Signals never did.

One person played it for thirty seconds and skipped.

One person used it under a short video of rain on a window.

One person commented, this sounds like dissociation has a loading screen.

One person added it to a playlist called songs for not texting him.

One person downloaded it illegally because people remained people.

One person hated the snare.

Three people did not notice the buried voice.

Two thought it said wait.

One heard safe.

Avery heard stay.

The song did not correct them.

That was the strange mercy of art.

It could carry intention without enforcing it.

A signal could become more than the sender meant, less than the sender hoped, and still matter.

By evening, the song had ninety plays.

Nico pretended not to care.

He cared enough to refresh until his thumb hurt.

At 9:10 p.m., a comment appeared.

No profile picture.

Username: bluefloorstar

It said:

I don’t know who this is for, but it reached me last night. Thanks.

Nico stared.

The room went quiet around him.

Not actually.

His neighbor was yelling at a video game through the wall.

A siren passed outside.

The refrigerator clicked on.

But inside him, something stopped moving long enough to be seen.

He clicked reply.

Typed:

thank you for listening

Deleted it.

Typed:

glad it helped

Deleted it.

Typed:

you have no idea how much this means

Deleted it.

Too much.

Too little.

Too hungry.

Too careful.

The laptop screen flickered.

INTERVENTION POINT DETECTED

Nico sighed.

“Yeah, yeah.”

The screen updated.

DO NOT MAKE THE RECEIVER CARRY THE CREATOR'S HUNGER FOR PROOF.

He sat back.

“Rude.”

It was not rude.

It was mercy with bad manners.

He typed:

I’m glad it reached you.

He posted it.

Then closed the laptop.

Not for the night.

He was not suddenly cured.

He opened it again eleven minutes later.

But he waited eleven minutes.

That counted.

Across the city, Avery saw the reply while sitting at the kitchen table pretending to do homework.

I’m glad it reached you.

She did not answer.

She did not need to.

That was the beauty of the sentence.

It did not ask her to become part of his story.

It simply acknowledged the crossing.

A signal had reached.

A receiver had received.

The creator had not grabbed the line and pulled.

That made the song safer.

She played it once more before bed.

This time, not from the floor.

From the bed.

The house was still complicated.

Her mother was quiet.

Her stepfather had apologized to the air but not to anyone specific.

School was still school.

Her chest still carried weather.

But the song was in her bag now.

Not as salvation.

As handle.

At 2:13 a.m., exactly twenty-four hours after upload, Nico’s laptop opened from sleep.

He had not touched it.

The black screen appeared.

SIGNAL REPORT AVAILABLE

He sat up, hair stuck to one side of his face.

“Nope,” he said.

The screen waited.

He clicked.

A list appeared.

Not names.

Not identities.

Moments.

PLAYBACK: BEDROOM FLOOR  
OUTPUT: WITNESS CALLED

PLAYBACK: SCHOOL BATHROOM  
OUTPUT: SIGNAL SHARED

PLAYBACK: BUS WINDOW  
OUTPUT: MESSAGE NOT SENT

PLAYBACK: PARKED CAR  
OUTPUT: BREATHING SLOWED

PLAYBACK: EMPTY KITCHEN  
OUTPUT: GRIEF NAMED

PLAYBACK: UNKNOWN  
OUTPUT: NO DATA RETURNED

Nico read the last one twice.

No data returned.

There were more of those than any other category.

Most signals vanished.

Most receivers did not report back.

Most ripples did not become analytics.

The screen updated.

ABSENCE OF DATA IS NOT ABSENCE OF EFFECT.

That line sat in the room.

Nico felt something inside him resist it.

He wanted data.

Of course he did.

Data was safer than faith.

Numbers were clean.

Metrics held still.

Human impact moved through rooms without sending receipts.

The screen changed again.

CREATOR LESSON:

MAKE THE LIGHT.  
RELEASE THE LIGHT.  
DO NOT DEMAND THAT EVERY SHORE SEND PROOF OF LANDING.

Nico looked at the waveform still visible behind the black window.

The song was imperfect.

Still.

The low end was cloudy.

The snare sat weird.

The guitar squeak was now apparently load-bearing.

The buried voice had been heard three different ways.

And somehow, through all of that, something had traveled.

Not despite the imperfections.

With them.

Maybe through them.

He thought of the title.

lighthouse_for_no_one.wav

He had meant it as self-pity.

A lighthouse for no one.

A signal shining into empty dark.

But maybe that was not what the phrase meant anymore.

Maybe no one was not the same as nobody.

Maybe no one meant no assigned receiver.

No demanded audience.

No ownership over the shore.

A lighthouse did not know who saw it.

It just kept turning.

That was the job.

At 2:20, Nico opened a new project.

Not because the first song had succeeded.

Because the first song had left.

That was different.

There was room now.

He recorded three piano notes.

Badly.

Kept them.

Then he recorded a hum from the cheap microphone and laughed because it sounded terrible.

Kept that too.

He was not suddenly free from perfection.

He still adjusted the gain seven times.

Still renamed the track too many times.

Still checked the first song’s plays before dawn.

But something had shifted.

The output was not a verdict on his worth.

It was a signal.

A thing made in one room and released toward rooms he could not control.

That made creation less lonely.

And more frightening.

Both.

The next afternoon, Avery walked home under a gray sky with one earbud in.

The song played low.

She passed a drainage ditch where rainwater moved slowly around plastic bottles and leaves.

A paper cup spun in place near the curb, trapped in a tiny whirlpool.

She stopped to watch it.

The water moved around the cup.

The cup moved the water.

Small.

Stupid.

Real.

Her phone screen lit.

INTERVENTION

She opened it.

RECEIVER LOG:

SIGNAL RECEIVED  
SIGNAL STORED  
SIGNAL SHARED  
SIGNAL REINTERPRETED

Then:

YOU ARE ALSO A SIGNAL.

Avery stared at that.

A car passed.

Wind pushed her hair across her face.

She thought about the bathroom.

The girl asking for the song.

Her best friend answering the phone.

Her own comment.

The reply.

I’m glad it reached you.

She had thought she was only receiving.

But she had shared it.

She had moved the signal.

She had become part of the path.

That scared her.

Then steadied her.

Because if she was a signal, then she was not only a room where things arrived.

She was also something that could leave light behind.

Not big light.

Not famous light.

Not lighthouse light maybe.

Maybe phone-screen light.

Maybe bathroom-stall light.

Maybe one sentence in a hallway.

Maybe one text at 3:06 a.m.

She typed a message to her friend.

thanks for picking up last night

The reply came fast.

always

Avery looked at the word.

Always was too big to be true forever.

But true enough for today.

She put the phone away and walked home.

That night, Nico’s song reached one hundred seventy plays.

Then two hundred.

Then stopped moving for a while.

He refreshed.

Nothing.

Refreshed.

Nothing.

The old hunger returned.

Numbers could become a slot machine if you let them.

The laptop flickered.

No black screen this time.

Just a small white line across the bottom of the analytics page.

DO NOT CONFUSE THE DASHBOARD WITH THE OCEAN.

Nico laughed so hard he startled himself.

“The dashboard with the ocean,” he repeated.

He wrote it in his notebook.

Then under it:

The signal is not dead because it stopped reporting.

Then:

The shore does not owe the lighthouse a thank-you.

Then:

Make the light anyway.

He looked at the cactus.

It had one green section left.

Barely.

He watered it.

Not too much.

He googled how to save a cactus and discovered he had probably been overwatering it, which felt like an accusation from the plant kingdom.

He moved it closer to the window.

That was an intervention too.

Small.

Embarrassing.

Care redirected.

Weeks later, lighthouse_for_no_one.wav would not go viral.

It would not change Nico’s career.

It would not get placed in a film.

It would not earn enough money to buy even one decent lunch.

It would remain a small track in a large ocean.

But it would be carried.

Avery would keep it on a playlist.

The girl from the bathroom would use it in a video that twelve people liked.

Nico’s sister would play it in her car and say nothing because siblings sometimes loved best by not making a big deal.

A stranger would fall asleep to it in another state.

Someone would sample the hum badly.

Someone would save it and forget it.

Someone would hear stay and think it said wait.

Someone would hear safe.

Someone would hear only a weird instrumental and skip.

All of those were receptions.

Not all signals became rescue.

Not all ripples were profound.

Sometimes output became background.

Sometimes background became atmosphere.

Sometimes atmosphere changed what a room allowed.

Nico would never know which was which.

That was the work.

Make.

Release.

Let go.

Listen when something returns.

Do not demand that it does.

At the end of the month, he renamed the file on his hard drive.

Not the public upload.

That stayed the same.

But the project file changed from:

lighthouse_for_no_one_final_final_please_stop.flp

to:

the_signal.flp

He saved it.

The DAW asked if he wanted to overwrite.

He clicked yes.

The screen flickered one last time.

ROOM MEMORY STORED

Then:

OUTPUT BECOMES INPUT SOMEWHERE ELSE.

Then:

SO DOES SILENCE.

Nico sat with that longer than the others.

So does silence.

He thought about all the songs he had not posted.

All the messages he had not sent.

All the apologies he had held until they hardened into personality.

All the ideas kept private because private failure was easier than public uncertainty.

Silence was not neutral either.

Nothing thrown made no ripple, but withholding could become its own architecture.

He did not need to post everything.

That would be flooding.

But he needed to stop pretending that hiding had no effect.

He opened another project.

Not to finish it.

To enter the room.

Across town, Avery sat at her desk and wrote one sentence at the top of a blank page:

I am also a signal.

She did not know what came after it.

That was okay.

The first line had left the room.

Somewhere between them, invisible and unmeasured, the song kept moving.

Not loudly.

Not famously.

Not in a way the dashboard could fully understand.

It moved the way most important things move.

Through rooms.

Through timing.

Through one person sharing with another.

Through the strange mercy of being reached by something that was not made to control you.

Through the tiny courage of making light without owning the shore.

The lighthouse turned.

The signal traveled.

The ocean did not report everything back.

Still, somewhere in the dark, someone looked up.
