# ARTIFACT_028_THE_GHOST_FROM_2007

## 👻 Field Note

The first lie was not even good.

That was the important part.

A good lie might have survived the porch light.

A good lie might have dressed itself in believable words, stood up straight, saluted authority, and asked the room to accept its paperwork.

This lie arrived wearing wet sneakers and holding a mutilated two-liter bottle.

Scott and Drew had been out back conducting what could only be described, generously, as experimental idiot science.

The apparatus was simple.

One bucket.

One bottle.

One backyard.

One teenage boy who knew exactly what he was doing.

One teenage boy who kept saying he did not feel anything yet.

That second boy was Drew.

Drew had never smoked before.

This was a tactical disadvantage.

Nobody told him the device was not entry-level equipment.

Nobody explained that a gravity bong is less of a casual introduction and more of a municipal water system launching your soul through a screen door.

Scott, being young and scientifically irresponsible, operated the machine with confidence.

Drew took the hit.

Drew waited.

Drew said, “I don’t feel it yet.”

The universe smiled.

Ten minutes later, Drew was laughing so hard he had become a public safety concern.

Not normal laughing.

Not polite laughing.

Not, “That was a funny joke, Scott,” laughing.

This was the kind of laughing that removes bones.

This was the kind of laughing where the person is no longer participating in language and has become a malfunctioning lawn decoration.

Scott, of course, found this hilarious.

Scott was also not doing great.

Neither of them heard the door at first.

Then Terry came outside.

Terry did not need evidence.

Evidence had already entered the air.

The backyard smelled like a skunk had joined a garage band.

There was a bucket on the ground.

There was a bottle in someone’s hand.

There were two boys attempting to look natural while clearly standing beside the least natural object in the county.

Terry looked at the bucket.

Then he looked at the bottle.

Then he looked at Scott.

“What are y’all doing?”

This was the moment.

Every courtroom has one.

Every suspect gets one clean chance to save himself.

Scott chose language.

He should not have.

He said, with the full confidence of a boy whose brain had just left the premises:

> “We’re testing my water-repelled rocket.”

There was a pause.

Not a normal pause.

A father pause.

A pause with experience in it.

A pause that had already smelled the crime, identified the weapon, assessed the suspects, and decided the paperwork was not worth it.

Terry stared at the alleged rocket.

The rocket did not look repelled by water.

The rocket looked like it had failed seventh-grade shop class.

Somewhere behind Scott, Drew made the terrible decision to continue existing while high for the first time.

This made not laughing nearly impossible.

Terry’s face did something almost holy.

He did not approve.

He did not explode.

He did not ask the obvious follow-up questions, because every follow-up question would have made the lie worse, and Terry had lived long enough to know when stupidity was already punishing itself.

His expression said several things at once.

It said:

> I know exactly what this is.

It said:

> That is not a rocket.

It said:

> You are dumber than a box of rocks.

It also said:

> Proceed, son.

Not out loud.

Terry was not that dramatic.

Out loud, he just accepted the impossible sentence as if backyard propulsion research was a normal family activity.

Then he went back inside.

The boys survived.

The rocket program continued briefly and accomplished absolutely nothing useful.

## 🚬 Porch Current

A few weeks later, the lie was retired.

There was no point hiding it anymore.

Terry knew.

Scott knew Terry knew.

Drew knew Terry knew.

The whole room knew.

After that, Terry would sometimes come outside and sit with them while they smoked.

Not joining.

Not preaching.

Just sitting.

Occupying the porch like a man who had decided that presence was better than performance.

He had lung cancer by then.

That mattered later.

At the time, it was just another fact in the yard.

The chairs were there.

The smoke was there.

The boys were there.

Terry was there.

And because Terry was still Terry, he waited for the perfect moment.

He waited until Scott was fully couch-locked.

He waited until Scott had become furniture with opinions.

Then Terry pointed at the cigarette butts scattered on the ground.

> “Pick up your cigarette butts.”

Scott heard this as persecution.

Scott heard this as injustice.

Scott heard this as a violation of the ancient teenage right to be left alone after becoming one with a lawn chair.

So Scott answered in the most exhausted, annoyed, loving, seventeen-year-old voice possible:

> “OKAY, DAD.”

And Terry, having successfully remained Terry, had won.

That was the whole memory.

Nothing exploded.

Nobody learned a lesson in the clean way lessons are advertised.

No angel appeared in the smoke.

No portal opened under the bucket.

No hidden agency recovered the bottle.

Just a sick father, a dumb kid, a laughing friend, a backyard, a bad lie, and cigarette butts on the ground.

## 🕯️ The Ghost Without The Ghost

Years later, the memory returned wrong.

Not wrong as in false.

Wrong as in impossible.

It came back after Thunderbird photos resurfaced.

It came back through Drew, who remembered too.

It came back after the laughter had already turned into grief and then somehow turned back into laughter again.

That is why it belongs in the Unexplained Layer.

Not because a ghost appeared.

Because one did not need to.

The ghost was already there.

It was in the sentence Scott heard in Terry’s voice after all these years:

> Cool rocket, dumbass.
>
> Pick up your cigarette butts.

The room changed after that.

Not dramatically.

Not loudly.

Just enough.

The dead do not always return as shadows.

Sometimes they return as a joke that still knows exactly where to stand.
