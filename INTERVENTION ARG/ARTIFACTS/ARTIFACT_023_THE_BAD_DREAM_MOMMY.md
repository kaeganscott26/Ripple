## The Bad Dream Mommy

I don’t tell people this story anymore.

Not because I’m afraid they won’t believe me. That part never bothered me much. People believe what they need to believe. If calling it sleep paralysis helps them sleep at night, fine. If saying “kids have nightmares” keeps their house feeling normal, I understand.

I don’t tell people because when I do, something in the room always changes.

Not dramatically.

The lights don’t flicker. The walls don’t bleed. There’s no cold breath on anyone’s neck.

It’s smaller than that.

The refrigerator stops humming for half a second.

A hallway seems longer than it did a minute before.

A door you’re sure you closed sits open just wide enough to notice.

And then, somewhere in the house, something makes a sound like a person trying to walk exactly like someone you love.

Almost.

That’s the word that matters.

Almost.

My earliest memory is not of my mother holding me.

It is not of my father laughing.

It is not of birthdays, toys, cartoons, or any of the soft little images people like to pretend childhood is made of.

My earliest memory is of dishes breaking in the kitchen.

I was still young enough to be in a crib.

Too young to understand rooms.

Too young to understand that a house has a layout, that walls divide one place from another, that sounds can travel strangely through vents and doorframes and old carpet.

But I understood fear.

The dishes started all at once.

Not one plate slipping from the counter.

Not a cup tipping over.

It was a crash. A whole cabinet of sound. Ceramic clapping against ceramic, glass shattering, silverware scattering across tile. It filled the house so suddenly that I remember gripping the crib rails before I even knew what hands were for.

Then it stopped.

That was worse.

A loud house can be explained.

A quiet house is waiting.

I remember lying there in the dark, staring at the bedroom door.

The room had a nightlight, I think. Maybe it was a hall light. Memory gets strange around terror. But I remember the door. I remember the carpet. I remember the little rectangle of black at the bottom where the room ended and the hallway began.

Then came the footsteps.

Fast.

Too fast.

Not running exactly.

Dragging.

Like someone was moving across the carpet without picking up their feet all the way.

Shff-shff-shff-shff-shff-shff—

Straight toward my room.

I couldn’t cry.

I couldn’t move.

I couldn’t even turn my head.

The door opened.

My mother looked in.

Only it wasn’t my mother.

I knew that before I had words for knowing.

She had my mother’s shape. My mother’s hair. My mother’s nightgown, maybe. Some part of her belonged to the woman who tucked me in, fed me, held me, said my name.

But her face was wrong.

Not monster-wrong. Not movie-wrong. Not fangs or claws or gray skin.

Worse.

Human wrong.

Like someone had drawn my mother from memory after only seeing her once through dirty glass.

Her eyes were too wide.

Her mouth was too still.

Her head sat strangely on her neck, tilted forward as if she had heard something inside my crib and was trying to decide whether to answer it.

She stood in the doorway and stared at me.

I stared back because there was nothing else my body would let me do.

After a while, she left.

The door stayed open.

I don’t know how long I lay there waiting for her to come back.

But she did.

That was the first thing I learned about the Bad Dream Mommy.

She came back.

I called her that because I was a child, and children give names to things adults are too afraid to describe.

The Bad Dream Mommy.

That was the closest I could get.

Because she was mommy-shaped.

Because she came from dreams.

Because she was bad.

Not mean.

Not angry.

Bad.

Like spoiled milk.

Like a dead outlet.

Like a smile on the wrong face.

She wasn’t there every night.

That would have been kinder, in a way. If a thing happens every night, it becomes a schedule. You can brace for it. You can build rituals around it. You can pretend predictability is safety.

The Bad Dream Mommy came when she wanted.

Always the same way.

First the kitchen.

Dishes crashing.

Then silence.

Then the carpet.

Fast dragging footsteps.

Then the door.

Sometimes the door opened.

Sometimes, if the door was already open, her face would slide into view from the hallway, just enough for me to see the eyes first.

Wide.

Wet.

Waiting.

Sometimes she stood in the doorway without moving.

Sometimes she leaned in.

Never all the way.

That was part of what made it unbearable.

She never rushed me.

She never climbed into the crib.

She never grabbed my ankle or whispered my name or did any of the things a normal nightmare would do.

She only watched.

Then she went away.

And sometimes, after I had just started to breathe again, the footsteps came back.

Shff-shff-shff-shff-shff—

The face returned to the doorway.

Still wrong.

Still almost my mother.

Still waiting for me to understand something I was too young to know.

The last time I saw the Bad Dream Mommy, I was eight.

By then, I knew what nightmares were. I knew what adults said when children told them impossible things. I knew how to watch someone’s face go soft with concern while their eyes quietly placed your story in a box labeled **dream**.

So I stopped telling people.

That is the second thing I learned.

If you tell people something is in the house, and they don’t believe you, then the house gets to keep it.

My father was the only person I ever felt safe with.

He had lost one of his legs in an accident when he was young. During the day he wore a prosthetic, and at night he used crutches. Because of that, he had a sound.

Everyone has a sound, if you love them enough.

A mother has the sound of cabinets opening in a certain order.

A father has the sound of clearing his throat before speaking.

A brother has the rhythm of his steps on stairs.

My father’s sound was metal and weight and patience.

Clink.

Pause.

Clunk.

Clink.

Pause.

Clunk.

His crutches touched the floor before he did. There was always a rhythm to it, always a small delay, always the careful movement of a man who had learned not to waste steps.

At night, when I was scared, I listened for that sound.

If I heard it, I was safe.

That is what I thought.

That is what a child should be allowed to think.

One night, before we moved into the house I still dream about, I was lying in my father’s bed while he went to the bathroom. I could see the bathroom door from where I was. I watched him go in.

I did not see him come out.

A minute passed.

Then I heard it from the other side of the apartment.

Clink.

Just once.

I sat up.

The bathroom light was still on.

My father was still in there. I knew he was. I had watched him go in, and I had not seen him come out.

Clink.

Again.

A little closer.

Not my father’s rhythm.

Too slow now.

Too deliberate.

A single metal tap every few minutes, each one nearer than the last.

Clink.

The sound moved through the apartment like it was testing the distance.

Clink.

I remember trying to call for my dad.

I couldn’t.

My throat locked.

The air around me got hot. Not warm. Hot. Like the room had filled with invisible steam. Like something had opened underneath the bed and the heat from it was rising through the mattress.

Clink.

It was right outside the room.

I stared at the doorway.

For a moment, nothing happened.

Then something stepped where I could see it.

It looked like my father.

Almost.

That word again.

It had his shape. His height. The suggestion of crutches. The outline of a man I trusted more than anything in the world.

But the body was wrong.

The face was wrong.

Not melted. Not injured. Not something clean enough to explain.

Deformed.

As if whatever had copied him had understood the outline but not the reason for the parts.

I froze.

The heat got worse.

The thing that looked like my father stood there with his wrong weight and his wrong face and his almost-crutches.

Then I screamed.

The next thing I remember, my real father was shaking me awake.

He was yelling my name.

His face was terrified.

Not angry. Not annoyed. Terrified.

I think that scared me more than the thing did.

Adults are not supposed to be afraid of what children see.

A few years later, we moved into the house.

I won’t say where it was. Not exactly. I used to think addresses mattered, like evil needed a street number. It doesn’t. Houses like that can sit anywhere. They can have trimmed lawns, attached garages, good school districts, working sprinklers, mailboxes with little flags, neighbors who wave.

From the outside, it was just a house.

From the inside, it was dark.

I don’t mean sunlight didn’t come through the windows. It did. I remember afternoon light on the kitchen floor. I remember bright rectangles on the carpet. I remember the TV glowing in the living room.

It wasn’t dark like absence of light.

It was dark like weight.

You felt it when you stepped inside.

The air in that house had hands.

The layout didn’t help.

There was a garage that opened into a laundry room. From there, you came into the kitchen and dining area. Off that side was a bedroom with its own bathroom, almost like a mother-in-law room. That became my room at first because it was big. Bigger than a kid needed. Bigger than I wanted.

My father’s office was off the kitchen, technically a second living room. The front hallway connected to the main living room, and beyond that was another hall with bedrooms. My father’s room was at the far end. There were guest rooms along the way.

At night, the house became a map of distances between me and him.

Every doorway mattered.

Every hall mattered.

Every room I had to cross alone was a dare.

I had nightmares constantly in that house.

Not normal bad dreams.

Heavy dreams. Dreams that left me with the feeling that something had been leaning over me and stepped away only when I opened my eyes.

Eventually, I begged to switch rooms.

I told my father I wanted to be closer to him.

That was true.

It was not the whole truth.

The whole truth was that my room felt like a place the house had chosen for me.

My father let me move into one of the smaller rooms down the hallway, closer to his. I had a little twin bed. I remember feeling safer there.

For a while.

Then came the night with the bathroom.

The room I moved into sat close to a bathroom that connected around the hallway. I don’t remember the exact architecture anymore, only the way sound came from where it shouldn’t have.

I woke up to a noise in that bathroom.

Something small.

A scrape maybe.

A bump.

Enough.

I yelled for my dad immediately.

He came down the hall on his crutches.

Clink.

Pause.

Clunk.

Clink.

Pause.

Clunk.

His real rhythm.

Slow. Tired. Familiar.

He came into my room and asked what was wrong. I told him I heard something. He looked around, checked what needed checking, and then, because he was my father and because I was scared, he lay down beside me in my twin bed.

I don’t know how we fit.

I only know that we did.

He was exhausted. He fell asleep quickly.

Soon he was snoring.

That sound should have been enough. My father asleep beside me. His weight in the bed. His breathing. His body between me and the door.

I stayed awake.

Fear does not sleep just because safety gets tired.

I lay there staring toward the hallway, listening to him snore.

Then, from his bedroom at the end of the hall, I heard his crutches.

Clink-clunk.

Fast.

Too fast.

Clink-clunk-clink-clunk-clink-clunk.

Coming toward my room.

My father was asleep beside me.

Snoring.

I did not move.

For one impossible second, my mind tried to solve it.

Maybe he wasn’t beside me.

But he was.

Maybe the sound wasn’t crutches.

But it was.

Maybe someone else was in the house.

But no one else walked with that sound.

Not exactly.

Almost.

The thing in the hallway was using my father’s sound, but it was impatient with it. It had learned the notes and not the song.

Clink-clunk-clink-clunk-clink-clunk—

Closer.

I screamed.

My father woke up hard, the way people do when fear is already in the room before their eyes open. He grabbed me. Asked what happened. Asked what I saw.

I hadn’t seen anything.

That was the worst part.

I didn’t need to.

I knew.

The house had learned him.

After that night, I changed.

I would not leave my father’s side.

Not for the bathroom.

Not for a shower.

Not to go get something from another room.

Not to sleep.

If he wanted me in bed at 8:30, then 8:30 had to become his bedtime too. If he stayed up later and tried to send me to bed alone, I cried. Not spoiled crying. Not tantrum crying.

Terror crying.

Because every time I heard him coming down the hallway after dark, I had to wait for the door to open before I knew whether it was really him.

That does something to a child.

It turns love into a test.

It turns footsteps into a question.

It turns every closed door into a mouth.

Years passed.

We moved.

The experiences faded, the way bruises fade if nothing keeps pressing on them.

The last time anything like that happened, I was thirteen.

I wish I could say there was a dramatic ending. A priest. A cleansing. A final confrontation. My father standing in the hallway with a baseball bat, telling the thing to get out of our house.

There wasn’t.

It just stopped.

Or maybe it learned something worse than how to mimic footsteps.

Maybe it learned patience.

I grew up.

I learned the words people use.

Sleep paralysis.

Night terrors.

Childhood anxiety.

Auditory hallucination.

Trauma response.

Hypnopompic imagery.

I don’t reject those words. They are useful words. They give shape to things. They help people talk without sounding insane.

But none of them explain the part I remember best.

The almost.

My mother, but not.

My father, but not.

The crashing dishes.

The dragging feet.

The crutches too fast in the hallway.

The way my body knew before my mind did.

People think the scariest sound is a scream.

It isn’t.

A scream tells you the danger has shown itself.

The scariest sound is someone you love moving through the house at the wrong time, from the wrong direction, with the wrong rhythm.

Because then you have to ask yourself:

If that is them…

who is beside me?

And if the person beside me is real…

what learned to sound like home?

I’m older now.

I live in places with lights I can control and doors I check twice. I don’t like long hallways. I don’t like sleeping with bedroom doors open. I don’t like hearing dishes shift in a sink after midnight.

And I do not answer when someone calls my name from another room unless I know exactly where everyone is.

That sounds paranoid until it saves you.

A few months ago, I visited a friend who had just moved into an old rental. Nothing special. One story. Attached garage. Laundry room off the kitchen. Dining area near a side bedroom.

I noticed the layout before I noticed I was afraid.

He laughed when I asked if the house ever felt heavy.

“Heavy?” he said.

“Never mind.”

We stayed up late talking. Around two in the morning, he went to shower. I sat on the couch in the living room, scrolling on my phone, trying not to listen to the pipes.

Then from the kitchen came a crash.

Dishes.

A whole cabinet of sound.

I stood up.

The shower was still running.

The house went quiet.

Not silent.

Waiting.

From the hallway carpet came a sound I had not heard since I was a child.

Shff-shff-shff-shff-shff—

Fast.

Dragging.

Coming closer.

I looked toward the hall.

At the far end, beside the guest room door, a face leaned slowly into view.

Wide eyes.

Still mouth.

A head tilted forward like it had heard something in me it remembered.

My friend’s voice called from the bathroom.

“You okay out there?”

The face in the hallway smiled.

Almost.

Then, using my mother’s voice, it whispered:

“Don’t answer him.”

The shower turned off.

My friend called again.

Closer now.

From behind the bathroom door.

“Hey. You okay?”

The thing in the hallway raised one finger to its wrong mouth.

And from the kitchen behind me, my father’s crutches tapped once against the tile.

Clink.
